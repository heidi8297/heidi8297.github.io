pane ONE: This is the story of nearly 9000 natural disasters that have occurred globally between 1960 and 2018.  If it feels like more disasters have occurred in recent years, your perception is correct.  The increasing frequency is troubling (particularly when broken down by type) as is the nature and impact of those events, and who is most affected by them. Let's take a look...

pane TWO: This data set chronicles disasters across 7 different categories shown here by total event count.  The most frequent types of disasters have been floods and storms.

pane THREE: Here is a look at how those events have played out across the globe.

pane THREE B: If we compare the first 10 years of the data set with the last 10 years, the trend is clear: disasters are occurring now more than ever. How long can we realistically sustain (survive) this?

pane FOUR: So what is the impact? Where are we seeing the largest loss of life? If we look at total death counts by event type, we can see that so far the largest death tolls have been from earthquakes and storms.

pane SIX: If we look now at the death toll for each individual event and how those tolls are distributed for each type, we can see which types of events are most likely to cause loss of life. The highest typical death toll is for "extreme temperature", which is particularly troubling when combined with the trends from the previous pane.
